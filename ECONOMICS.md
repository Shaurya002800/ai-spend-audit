# ECONOMICS.md

## What a lead might be worth

The basic business case behind this project is that if SpendLens identifies a company that is obviously overspending on AI tools, that company is more likely to be a strong Credex lead.

My rough thinking:

- a small-to-mid-sized team might be spending a few hundred dollars a month on AI tools
- if Credex helps that team buy equivalent credits at a discount, there is room for a meaningful margin
- not every audit matters equally, so the higher-savings cases are the ones that really count

I do not have real closed-loop revenue data here, so I treated this as directional math, not a claim of proven unit economics.

## Rough value range

For the purpose of this project, I think a qualified high-savings lead is probably worth more than a random email capture and less than a fully closed customer. So the useful question is whether the product can surface people who are clearly worth following up with.

If a team is saving hundreds per month and Credex can convert that into a long-term customer relationship, the lead could be fairly valuable. If the audit shows almost no savings, then the lead is much less useful even if the user completes the form.

## What would matter most

The funnel I would care about is:

`visitor -> audit completed -> high-savings result -> email captured -> consultation -> purchase`

The two most important pieces are:

- how often the app produces a genuinely compelling savings result
- whether those users actually take the next step

If the app generates lots of audits but very few strong savings cases, it might still be a decent content or awareness asset, but it would not be a strong lead-gen tool. If it surfaces real waste consistently, then even modest traffic could be enough to make it worthwhile.

## My honest take

I think the economics are plausible enough for a project like this, but still unproven. The concept makes sense because the pain is specific and measurable, but the real test would be whether users trust the result enough to act on it.

For me, the point of the project was less "I proved a full business" and more "I built a version that could realistically be tested."
